# MQI Future Vision

## Product thesis
Manufacturing Quote Intelligence is not just a quote generator. It is a manufacturing decision platform that turns fragmented commercial, operational, and supply-side inputs into faster, safer quoting decisions with stronger margin protection and clearer fulfillment confidence.

## Problem statement
Today’s quoting process depends on manual reconstruction across source files, supplier replies, internal approvals, cost assumptions, and delivery constraints. As supplier conditions, labor, material availability, and logistics shift, humans are forced to make high-stakes quote decisions with incomplete and stale information.

## Long-term strategy
MQI starts with quoting, but the long-term wedge is broader manufacturing decision intelligence. The platform is intended to:
- normalize inbound RFQ and order requirements
- evaluate feasible manufacturing paths across multiple manufacturers
- compare capacity, inventory, lead time, quality, logistics, and margin posture
- recommend the strongest manufacturing path with transparent rationale
- expand from quoting into wider sourcing, supplier, and fulfillment intelligence

## Future-state platform vision
The preserved future-state vision supports:
- multi-manufacturer orchestration
- capacity routing and allocation
- inventory-aware decisioning
- fulfillment routing and distribution logic
- manufacturing network intelligence
- quote recommendation across multiple candidate manufacturers
- long-horizon platform expansion beyond a single quoting workflow

## Multi-manufacturer architecture
The current preserved prototype and deck assume a qualified manufacturer set can be evaluated in parallel. The system compares candidate manufacturers across:
- capacity availability
- inventory readiness
- lead-time fit
- quality confidence
- commercial risk
- logistics risk
- margin contribution

That architecture supports both immediate quote recommendation and later network-level optimization.

## Manufacturing network intelligence
The future-state platform is positioned to grow from manufacturer comparison into network intelligence, including:
- supplier performance visibility
- sourcing intelligence
- pricing intelligence
- predictive recommendations
- network-aware routing decisions

## Capacity allocation
The preserved evaluation flow already frames capacity as a first-class decision signal. In the future-state model, capacity is not a passive reference field; it becomes an allocatable constraint across multiple manufacturers and order scenarios.

## Inventory optimization
Inventory is treated as part of quote confidence and readiness. In the future-state model, inventory data evolves into a broader optimization input that influences:
- quote timing credibility
- manufacturer selection
- replenishment risk
- margin sensitivity
- delivery confidence

## Fulfillment routing
The future-state platform extends beyond quote generation toward fulfillment-aware path selection. This includes routing logic that weighs production, transit, timing, and downstream delivery commitments.

## Multi-manufacturer quote scenarios
The preserved prototype currently demonstrates candidate comparison and best-path recommendation. In the future-state model, MQI supports scenarios such as:
- balanced commercial recommendation
- margin-optimized recommendation
- logistics-prioritized recommendation
- alternate manufacturer comparison under changing supply conditions
- exception-handling when preferred paths fail timing, inventory, or risk thresholds

## Order distribution
Longer term, MQI can support distribution of work across a qualified manufacturing network when a single manufacturer is not the best fit for all commercial or fulfillment conditions.

## Long-term roadmap summary
1. Quoting workflow intelligence
2. Multi-manufacturer recommendation engine
3. Manufacturing network intelligence
4. Distributed fulfillment and optimization platform

## Preservation note
This document preserves the future-state platform direction exactly as strategic scope. It is not an MVP brief and should not be simplified to match the current single-manufacturer production posture.
